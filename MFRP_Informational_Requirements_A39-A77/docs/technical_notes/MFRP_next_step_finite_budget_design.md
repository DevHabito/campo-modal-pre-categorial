# Finite-Budget Minimax Transform Design

**Programme:** Modal Field Research Programme  
**Provisional audit:** A42  
**Author line:** Felipe Gianini Romero  
**Status:** rigorous mathematical continuation of MFRP-TR-2026-01 and A39–A41; no physical design claim

## Technical abstract

This note formulates a finite-budget parameter-selection problem for exponential observations.
The contract is explicit:

- microscopic support is known to lie in \(\{0,1,2,3,4,5\}\);
- the mean is known exactly and equals \(5/2\);
- the omitted target is \(L(\log2)\);
- three observed transform parameters must be selected;
- the candidate catalogue is
  \[
  \{2\log2,3\log2,4\log2,5\log2,6\log2\};
  \]
- all selected observations have a common absolute tolerance \(\varepsilon\);
- the risk is the worst possible width of the omitted Laplace-transform interval over all
  compatible reported data.

For a design \(D\), the minimax risk is

\[
R_\varepsilon(D)
=
\sup_b
\left[
\max_{P\in\mathcal F_D(b,\varepsilon)}L_P(\log2)
-
\min_{P\in\mathcal F_D(b,\varepsilon)}L_P(\log2)
\right].
\]

It is equivalent to one finite linear programme over two probability vectors:

\[
R_\varepsilon(D)
=
\max_{P,Q}
\left[
L_P(\log2)-L_Q(\log2)
\right]
\]

subject to equal normalization and mean and

\[
|L_P(k\log2)-L_Q(k\log2)|\le2\varepsilon
\quad\text{for every }k\in D.
\]

All ten three-parameter designs are exhaustively evaluated with exact rational primal and
dual certificates.

With exact observations, the unique minimax design in the declared catalogue is

\[
D_0^\star=\{2,3,4\},
\]

with risk

\[
R_0(D_0^\star)=\frac{105}{44432}
\approx0.002363161685271876.
\]

At the A41 benchmark tolerance

\[
\varepsilon=10^{-4},
\]

the unique minimax design becomes

\[
D_{10^{-4}}^\star=\{2,3,6\},
\]

with risk

\[
R_{10^{-4}}(D_{10^{-4}}^\star)
=
\frac{12851927671}{2974447980000}
\approx0.004320777420689670.
\]

Thus exact-data optimality and noise-aware optimality differ. The exact-data winner is about
\(14.45\%\) worse than \(\{2,3,4\}\) at zero tolerance, but at \(\varepsilon=10^{-4}\) the
noise-aware winner improves the minimax transform width by about \(10.25\%\) relative to
\(\{2,3,4\}\).

The result is finite-catalogue and contract-relative. It does not prove a universally optimal
continuous parameter grid, an optimal design for the nonlinear \(Q\)-width, or any physical
measurement prescription.

## 1. Declared design problem

Let the known support be

\[
S=\{0,1,2,3,4,5\}.
\]

For a probability vector \(p=(p_0,\ldots,p_5)\), define

\[
L_p(k\log2)=\sum_{x=0}^5p_x2^{-kx}.
\]

The target is

\[
T(p)=L_p(\log2)=\sum_{x=0}^5p_x2^{-x}.
\]

The admissible microscopic class is

\[
\mathcal P_{5/2}
=
\left\{
p\ge0:
\sum_xp_x=1,\quad
\sum_xxp_x=\frac52
\right\}.
\]

The candidate exponent catalogue is

\[
\mathcal C=\{2,3,4,5,6\}.
\]

A design is a three-element subset

\[
D\subset\mathcal C,\qquad |D|=3.
\]

There are exactly

\[
\binom53=10
\]

candidate designs.

For reported values \(b=(b_k)_{k\in D}\) and common absolute tolerance
\(\varepsilon\ge0\), define

\[
\mathcal F_D(b,\varepsilon)
=
\left\{
p\in\mathcal P_{5/2}:
|L_p(k\log2)-b_k|\le\varepsilon
\quad\forall k\in D
\right\}.
\]

The declared minimax transform-width risk is

\[
R_\varepsilon(D)
=
\sup_{b:\mathcal F_D(b,\varepsilon)\ne\varnothing}
\left[
\max_{p\in\mathcal F_D(b,\varepsilon)}T(p)
-
\min_{p\in\mathcal F_D(b,\varepsilon)}T(p)
\right].
\]

This risk is distribution-free inside the declared support and mean class. It does not use a
prior distribution over microscopic states or reported data.

## 2. Pairwise linear-programme reduction

### Theorem 2.1 — minimax-width equivalence

For every design \(D\),

\[
R_\varepsilon(D)
=
\max_{p,q\in\mathcal P_{5/2}}
\left[
T(p)-T(q)
\right]
\]

subject to

\[
|L_p(k\log2)-L_q(k\log2)|
\le2\varepsilon
\quad\forall k\in D.
\]

### Proof

If \(p\) and \(q\) belong to the same reported-data box centered at \(b\), then

\[
|L_p(k\log2)-L_q(k\log2)|
\le
|L_p(k\log2)-b_k|
+
|L_q(k\log2)-b_k|
\le2\varepsilon.
\]

Thus every interval width generated by one reported box is feasible in the pairwise problem.

Conversely, suppose \(p,q\in\mathcal P_{5/2}\) satisfy the pairwise inequalities. Set

\[
b_k=
\frac12
\left[
L_p(k\log2)+L_q(k\log2)
\right].
\]

Then both \(p\) and \(q\) lie within \(\varepsilon\) of \(b_k\). Hence they belong to one
common feasible reported-data box. Taking suprema proves equality. \(\square\)

Because the support is finite, the pairwise problem is a linear programme in twelve
nonnegative variables.

## 3. Exact dual certification

Write

\[
z=(p_0,\ldots,p_5,q_0,\ldots,q_5).
\]

The primal objective is

\[
c^\top z=T(p)-T(q).
\]

At \(\varepsilon=0\), the observed-transform differences are equality constraints. At
positive tolerance, they are paired inequalities.

For every candidate design and benchmark, the audit supplies:

1. an exact rational primal feasible point;
2. exact normalization and mean verification;
3. exact transform equality or tolerance verification;
4. an exact rational dual feasible point;
5. nonnegative reduced costs;
6. exact equality between primal and dual objectives.

Therefore every reported design risk is globally optimal for its declared linear programme.
No ranking is inferred from floating-point optimization alone.

## 6. Why the optimal design changes

The exact-data design \(\{2,3,4\}\) has the smallest structural ambiguity at
\(\varepsilon=0\). However, its ambiguity grows more rapidly under a common tolerance than
the ambiguity of designs using a farther parameter.

On their initial common active-set regimes, the three leading risks are affine:

\[
R_\varepsilon(\{2,3,4\})
=
\frac{18375+191091968\varepsilon}{7775600},
\]

\[
R_\varepsilon(\{2,3,5\})
=
\frac{504525+3515417344\varepsilon}{194921552},
\]

\[
R_\varepsilon(\{2,3,6\})
=
\frac{861273+5147406080\varepsilon}{318430224}.
\]

Their initial slopes are approximately

\[
24.57585,\qquad18.03504,\qquad16.16494.
\]

Thus the exact-data winner is also the most tolerance-sensitive of these three designs.

The pairwise affine crossover values are

\[
\varepsilon_{234,235}
=
\frac{2522625}{73272409216}
\approx3.4428034058\times10^{-5},
\]

\[
\varepsilon_{235,236}
=
\frac{26699463}{428965935488}
\approx6.2241452738\times10^{-5}.
\]

These crossover equations explain the direction of the design change. They are not presented
as a complete global phase diagram for every tolerance, because active sets of some candidate
programmes change as \(\varepsilon\) increases.

## 7. Performance comparison

At exact data,

\[
\frac{R_0(\{2,3,6\})}{R_0(\{2,3,4\})}-1
\approx14.45\%.
\]

So choosing the noisy-data winner when the data are exact would sacrifice about \(14.45\%\)
in minimax transform resolution.

At \(\varepsilon=10^{-4}\),

\[
1-
\frac{R_{10^{-4}}(\{2,3,6\})}
{R_{10^{-4}}(\{2,3,4\})}
\approx10.25\%.
\]

So retaining the exact-data design at that tolerance would enlarge the worst-case transform
interval by about \(10.25\%\) relative to the noise-aware design.

This is a genuine robustness tradeoff, not a numerical-ranking accident: every table entry
has an exact primal-dual certificate.

## 8. Relation to future-score uncertainty

The optimized risk concerns the additive transform \(L(\log2)\), not directly the nonlinear
score

\[
Q_{\log2}=-\log_2L(\log2).
\]

Under the mean constraint \(5/2\) on the six-point support,

\[
\frac{3}{16}\le L(\log2)\le\frac{33}{64}.
\]

Therefore a transform-width guarantee \(R\) implies the universal score-difference bound

\[
|Q_{\log2}(p)-Q_{\log2}(q)|
\le
\log_2\left(1+\frac{16R}{3}\right).
\]

For a centered contraction with \(a=1/2\), the corresponding future-score width obeys

\[
|Q'_{2\log2}(p)-Q'_{2\log2}(q)|
\le
\frac12
\log_2\left(1+\frac{16R}{3}\right).
\]

For the two selected designs, this gives the certified outer bounds

\[
\Delta Q'_{2\log2}
\le
0.00903470862684458
\]

at exact data, and

\[
\Delta Q'_{2\log2}
\le
0.01643420069051995
\]

at \(\varepsilon=10^{-4}\).

These are induced bounds. The design minimizing transform width need not be the design
minimizing the exact nonlinear \(Q\)-width. That is a separate optimization problem.

## 9. Logical status

### Established

1. The worst reported-box transform width is exactly reducible to one pairwise linear
   programme.
2. Every one of the ten candidate designs has an exact rational primal-dual optimum at
   \(\varepsilon=0\) and \(\varepsilon=10^{-4}\).
3. \(\{2,3,4\}\) is the unique exact-data minimax transform design in the declared catalogue.
4. \(\{2,3,6\}\) is the unique minimax transform design at the declared noisy benchmark.
5. Exact-data and noise-aware designs can differ.
6. The top designs exhibit an explicit structural-resolution versus noise-sensitivity tradeoff.
7. Transform-width risk yields a rigorous, but generally nonsharp, future-score-width bound.

### Not established

1. The candidate catalogue is not proved physically or mathematically unique.
2. No continuous optimization over arbitrary positive parameters has been solved.
3. No complete tolerance phase diagram has been proved.
4. The chosen design minimizes transform width, not exact nonlinear \(Q\)-width.
5. No measurement cost depending on parameter magnitude has been included.
6. Equal absolute tolerance is a declared benchmark, not an empirical noise law.
7. The support and mean contracts remain formal.

## 10. Next rigorous target

The next step should remove one remaining simplification:

\[
\text{optimize the exact nonlinear future-score width itself}.
\]

For a design \(D\), define

\[
\mathcal R_\varepsilon^{Q}(D)
=
\sup_{p,q}
\left|
Q'_{2\log2}(p)-Q'_{2\log2}(q)
\right|
\]

under the same support, mean, and pairwise observation constraints.

This is a linear-fractional or quasiconvex problem because

\[
Q_{\log2}(p)-Q_{\log2}(q)
=
\log_2\frac{L_q(\log2)}{L_p(\log2)}.
\]

It should be solved and certified separately. The current A42 result should not be silently
promoted into that stronger design claim.
