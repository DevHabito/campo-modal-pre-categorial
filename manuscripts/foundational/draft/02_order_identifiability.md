# 2. Order-Only Identifiability

## 2.1 Relational observation

Let \(I=\{1,\ldots,n\}\) be a finite labeled set. A relational observation is a
binary relation

\[
R\subseteq I\times I.
\]

The discussion in this section applies most directly when \(R\) is obtained
from a latent coordinatewise order. Let

\[
Z_i=(X_i^{(1)},\ldots,X_i^{(d)})\in\mathbb R^d
\]

and define

\[
i\preceq_R j
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
X_i^{(r)}\le X_j^{(r)}
\ \text{for every }r=1,\ldots,d.
\]

The relation records which labeled pairs are comparable in every coordinate.
It does not record the numerical coordinate values, the units used for those
coordinates, or the marginal sampling densities. This distinction is not a
defect of the representation: it is the information content of the
representation.

The central question is therefore an identifiability question. Which latent
quantities are constant on every class of coordinate models that produce the
same observed relation? Any quantity that varies inside such a class cannot be
recovered by a deterministic estimator whose input is only \(R\) and \(n\).

## 2.2 Monotone recalibration

**Lemma 2.1 (coordinatewise monotone invariance).**  
For each coordinate \(r\), let

\[
T_r:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R
\]

be strictly increasing. Set

\[
\widetilde X_i^{(r)}=T_r(X_i^{(r)}).
\]

Then the coordinatewise order generated by
\(\widetilde Z_i=(\widetilde X_i^{(1)},\ldots,\widetilde X_i^{(d)})\) is
identical to the order generated by \(Z_i\).

**Proof.** Strict monotonicity gives

\[
X_i^{(r)}\le X_j^{(r)}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
T_r(X_i^{(r)})\le T_r(X_j^{(r)})
\]

for every coordinate \(r\). Taking the conjunction over all coordinates
preserves every relation entry. Hence the two labeled relation matrices are
equal. \(\square\)

The lemma is elementary, but its consequence is substantial. The
transformations \(T_r\) can strongly alter coordinate spacings and marginal
densities while leaving the observed order unchanged. Therefore numerical
density, marginal scale, and coordinate spacing are not functions of the
order alone.

This statement should not be confused with the stronger claim that order
contains no geometric information. An order can encode rich invariant
structure. The point is narrower: information that changes under
order-preserving recalibration is not identifiable from the order without an
additional convention or observable.

## 2.3 Copula dependence

Suppose now that the latent vectors are independent and identically
distributed, and consider the two-dimensional case for clarity. Let
\((X_i,Y_i)\), \(i=1,\ldots,n\), have continuous strictly increasing marginal
distribution functions \(F_X\) and \(F_Y\). Define

\[
i\prec_R j
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
X_i<X_j\ \text{and}\ Y_i<Y_j.
\]

Set

\[
U_i=F_X(X_i),
\qquad
V_i=F_Y(Y_i).
\]

By strict monotonicity,

\[
X_i<X_j\Longleftrightarrow U_i<U_j,
\qquad
Y_i<Y_j\Longleftrightarrow V_i<V_j
\]

almost surely. The probability-integral transform therefore couples the
original sample to a sample with uniform marginals and exactly the same
labeled order. By Sklar's theorem, the joint law of \((U,V)\) is the copula of
\((X,Y)\) [@Sklar1959].

**Proposition 2.2 (copula sufficiency for the induced order law).**  
Under continuous strictly increasing marginals, the probability law of the
finite labeled coordinatewise order depends on the latent joint distribution
only through its copula.

**Proof.** Under the coupling above, the complete labeled relation matrix
constructed from \((X_i,Y_i)\) is equal almost surely to the matrix constructed
from \((U_i,V_i)\). The latter has uniform marginals and the same copula.
Consequently every event measurable from the finite order has a probability
determined by the copula. \(\square\)

The proposition says “depends at most on the copula.” It does not assert that
different copulas always induce different order laws at every finite sample
size. That converse would require a separate injectivity result.

## 2.4 An order-only identifiability limit

Let \(\mathcal M\) be a class of latent models, and let

\[
\Phi:\mathcal M\to\mathcal R_n
\]

map a latent model to its observed labeled relation. Let
\(\theta:\mathcal M\to\Theta\) be a target quantity.

**Proposition 2.3 (fiberwise identifiability criterion).**  
A deterministic estimator \(T:\mathcal R_n\to\Theta\) satisfying

\[
T(\Phi(M))=\theta(M)
\quad\text{for every }M\in\mathcal M
\]

can exist only if \(\theta\) is constant on every fiber of \(\Phi\).

**Proof.** If \(\Phi(M_1)=\Phi(M_2)\), then determinism implies

\[
T(\Phi(M_1))=T(\Phi(M_2)).
\]

Therefore exact recovery requires \(\theta(M_1)=\theta(M_2)\). \(\square\)

Combining this criterion with Lemma 2.1 yields the order-only obstruction:
marginal scales, marginal densities, and any other quantities altered by
strict coordinatewise recalibration cannot be recovered from a single finite
relation matrix and its cardinality. Proposition 2.2 extends the conclusion
to repeated independent samples: two models with the same copula and different
continuous marginals induce the same law for every finite sequence of observed
orders, so order-only statistical procedures cannot consistently distinguish
those marginal models.

This is an identifiability statement, not a universal impossibility claim.
The obstruction can be removed by enriching the observation. Examples include
a calibrated coordinate, an independently specified measure, physically
distinguished marks, repeated observations containing non-order data, or a
dynamical rule whose observables are not invariant under the same
recalibrations.

## 2.5 Consequence for relational model design

The order \(R\) should be treated as one informational layer rather than a
complete state description. It can support ordinal questions—comparability,
reachability, precedence, intervals, and quotient structure—but it does not
supply a preferred cardinal scale or sampling measure.

This motivates the separation adopted in the remainder of the manuscript.
A quantitative mark \(q\) is introduced only when cardinal contrasts are
required; an additive mass \(\mu\) is introduced only when refinement and
multiplicity must be tracked; a transition kernel \(K\) is introduced only
when stochastic evolution is specified; and an occupancy \(\pi\) is introduced
only when microscopic transitions must be aggregated dynamically. None of
these structures follows merely from renaming the order.

The conclusion is methodological:

\[
\boxed{
\text{before deriving an observable from }R,\text{ test whether it is constant
on the order-equivalence classes.}
}
\]

If it is not, the observable requires additional structure.
